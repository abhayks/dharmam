# Sankhya Yoga (The Yoga of Knowledge)

## Introduction

Chapter 2 marks the true beginning of the Bhagavad Gita's teachings. After Arjuna surrenders completely, casting aside his arguments and accepting Lord Krishna as his spiritual master, the divine discourse begins. Krishna starts by addressing the root of Arjuna's grief—his attachment and ignorance about the true nature of the Self. This chapter lays the philosophical foundation for the entire Gita, introducing concepts of the eternal soul, selfless action, and the state of perfect equanimity.

![Krishna and Arjuna](/images/krishna-arjuna-chariot.jpg)

## Chapter Summary

Seeing Arjuna overwhelmed with pity and sorrow, Krishna first rebukes him for his "unmanly" weakness, urging him to cast off this faint-heartedness and arise. When Arjuna reiterates his confusion and formally surrenders as Krishna's disciple, the Lord begins his profound teaching.

Krishna explains that the wise grieve for neither the living nor the dead. He introduces the core concept of **Sankhya philosophy**: the distinction between the eternal, unchanging Soul (Atman) and the temporary, perishable body. The soul is unborn, immortal, and indestructible; it merely casts off old bodies and acquires new ones, like a person changing worn-out clothes.

He then reminds Arjuna of his **Svadharma** (prescribed duty) as a Kshatriya (warrior), stating that for a warrior, there is no greater good than a righteous war. To shrink from this duty would bring infamy and sin.

Finally, Krishna introduces the path of **Karma Yoga** (the yoga of selfless action), also known as **Buddhi Yoga**. He instructs Arjuna to perform his duty with detachment, without any concern for the fruits of his actions—success or failure. The chapter concludes with Arjuna asking about the qualities of a person whose wisdom is steady (`Sthitaprajna`). Krishna describes this enlightened person as one who is free from desires, attachment, fear, and anger, and who remains unshaken by joy or sorrow.

## Key Themes

*   **The Immortal Soul (Atman):** The core teaching that the soul is eternal and cannot be slain.
*   **The Perishable Body:** The body is merely a temporary vessel for the soul.
*   **Svadharma (Duty):** The importance of fulfilling one's own prescribed duty in society.
*   **Karma Yoga (Selfless Action):** The principle of working without attachment to the results.
*   **Sthitaprajna (The Person of Steady Wisdom):** The ideal state of a self-realized soul with a stable mind.

## Significance of the Chapter

This chapter is arguably the most important in the Gita as it contains a summary of its entire philosophy. It directly addresses Arjuna's delusion by establishing the eternal nature of the Self, thus removing the cause for his grief. It introduces the two primary paths to liberation that will be expanded upon later: **Sankhya** (the path of knowledge) and **Karma Yoga** (the path of action).
