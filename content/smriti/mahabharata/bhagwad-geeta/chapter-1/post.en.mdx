# The Yoga of Arjuna's Despondency

## Introduction

The first chapter of the Srimad Bhagavad Gita, known as 'Arjuna Vishada Yoga', serves as the prologue to the great war of Mahabharata. It begins on the battlefield of Kurukshetra, with two massive armies arrayed against each other. This chapter is not merely a description of a battle; it is the story of the moral and emotional conflict arising in the heart of a great warrior.

![Kurukshetra Battlefield](/images/kurukshetra.jpg)

## Chapter Summary

The chapter opens with the blind king Dhritarashtra asking his secretary, Sanjaya, what his sons and the sons of Pandu did after assembling on the holy field of Kurukshetra. Sanjaya, who has been granted divine vision by the sage Vyasa, begins to narrate the events from the battlefield.

Arjuna, the great Pandava hero, asks his charioteer, Lord Krishna, to drive their chariot into the middle of the two armies. He wishes to see the warriors he is about to fight. When Krishna positions the chariot before Bhishma, Drona, and other kinsmen, Arjuna's heart is overwhelmed. Seeing his own family members, teachers, and friends as his enemies, he sinks into a state of profound grief, compassion, and attachment. His body trembles, his mouth goes dry, and his legendary bow, the Gandiva, slips from his hands.

## Key Themes

*   **Sorrow and Compassion:** Arjuna's attachment to his loved ones makes him averse to his duty as a warrior.
*   **Moral Dilemma (Dharma-Sankat):** Arjuna is caught in a deep moral crisis between his duty (Kshatriya Dharma) and his familial love.
*   **The Effect of Attachment (Moha):** This chapter demonstrates how excessive attachment and delusion can divert a person from their righteous path.

## Arjuna's Arguments

Overcome with grief, Arjuna presents several arguments to Sri Krishna against fighting the war:
1.  **Compassion:** How can I kill my own relatives?
2.  **Lack of Joy:** What is the use of enjoying a kingdom obtained through such bloodshed?
3.  **Fear of Sin:** Killing one's own kinsmen will only bring sin.
4.  **Destruction of the Family:** The war will lead to the destruction of the family, which in turn will destroy timeless family traditions and righteousness.
5.  **Corruption of Lineage (Varna-sankara):** With the destruction of the family, the women may become corrupted, leading to the rise of an unwanted populace that drags the family and society to hell.

After presenting these arguments, Arjuna casts aside his bow and arrows and sits down on the seat of the chariot, his mind overwhelmed with sorrow.

## Significance of the Chapter

This chapter is not just an introduction; it creates the psychological stage upon which the divine knowledge of the Gita is revealed. Arjuna's 'Vishada' or despondency is the very catalyst that compels Lord Krishna to deliver his celestial sermon on Karma (action), Jnana (knowledge), and Bhakti (devotion). It teaches us that life's greatest confusions and crises can often lead us toward the deepest truths.

## Key Verses

> Dhritarashtra said: O Sanjaya, after my sons and the sons of Pandu assembled in the place of pilgrimage at Kurukshetra, desiring to fight, what did they do? (1.1)

> My whole body is trembling, my hair is standing on end, my bow Gandiva is slipping from my hand, and my skin is burning. (1.29)

> Sanjaya said: Having spoken thus, Arjuna, casting aside his bow and arrows, sat down on the seat of the chariot, his mind overwhelmed with sorrow. (1.47)
